Does a law exist in the US that allows patents to be classified if they pose a threat or are of interest to the state?

  • When was this law passed?
  • How many patents have been classified under this law?

ü§ñ AI Answer:

Yes, that's correct. In the United States, there is a law that allows the government to classify patent applications and even already issued patents if it is believed that their disclosure could harm national security.

Invention Secrecy Act of 1951

This law is called the **Invention Secrecy Act of 1951**, codified as 35 U.S. Code ¬ß 181.

When was the Invention Secrecy Act passed?

The history of this law dates back to 1917, during the First World War, when the first similar act was passed. However, its current version, the "Invention Secrecy Act," was adopted in 1951 at the height of the Cold War.

How it works?

When a patent application is submitted to the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO), it undergoes an initial review. If the invention relates to technologies that could potentially have military or strategic significance (e.g., nuclear energy, defense systems, cryptography, new materials, or communication methods), the USPTO can refer it to the relevant defense or intelligence agencies for review (e.g., Department of Defense, NSA).

If these agencies conclude that the publication or disclosure of the invention (by publishing the application or issuing the patent) "could be detrimental to national security," the Commissioner for Patents can issue a "Secrecy Order."

This order:

  • Prohibits the publication of the patent application.
  • Delays the issuance of the patent.
  • Restricts the inventor's disclosure of the invention (prohibits the sale, export, or discussion of the invention without permission).
  • Requires the documents related to the invention to be kept confidential.
  • Is usually extended annually, unless there is a declared national emergency or war, in which case it remains in effect for the duration of the emergency plus six months.

It is important to note that such an order can be imposed even on inventions developed by private individuals or companies without any government funding or participation.

How many patents are classified?

The exact number of classified patents is not publicly available, as it is itself confidential. However, the Federation of American Scientists (FAS), which deals with government secrecy issues, regularly publishes data obtained from the USPTO.

According to their reports:

  • At the end of the 2017 fiscal year (September 30, 2017), there were 5,784 active secrecy orders in the United States.
  • At the end of the 2018 fiscal year (September 30, 2018), this figure increased slightly to 5,792 orders. This was the highest figure since 1993.

These figures show that the practice of classifying patents not only exists but is also actively used, and the number of such orders remains significant. Dozens of new secrecy orders are issued annually, while a smaller number of old ones are canceled.

Thus, the "Invention Secrecy Act of 1951" is a powerful tool in the hands of the U.S. government to control the dissemination of technologies that could pose a threat to national security, and it is actively applied to thousands of inventions.

üí¨ Question:

Who determines that a patent poses a threat to US national security for its classification?

  • Could this be a conflict of interest disguised as a threat?
  • Is there a judicial procedure for appealing or appealing such decisions?

ü§ñ AI Answer:

Indeed, the question of who makes the decision to classify a patent and whether there are mechanisms for challenging it is extremely important, especially given the potential for "conflicts of interest."

Who decides that it is a "threat"?

The decision to impose a Secrecy Order is not made by one person, but as a result of a process involving several federal agencies:

  • USPTO (United States Patent and Trademark Office): All patent applications submitted to the USPTO undergo an initial review to check for sensitive information that may be relevant to national security. This review is carried out by specially appointed employees.
  • "Defense Agencies": If potentially sensitive information is identified during the initial review at the USPTO, the patent application is referred to one or more relevant "defense agencies" for review. These agencies are determined by the US President and include, but are not limited to:
    • Department of Defense (DoD)
    • National Security Agency (NSA)
    • Department of Energy (DoE), especially on issues related to nuclear technologies.
    • NASA (National Aeronautics and Space Administration).
    • Department of Homeland Security.
    • Department of Justice.

    In general, any federal government agency that has the right to classify data can request that a patent be classified.

  • Commissioner for Patents: If one of these defense agencies concludes that the publication or disclosure of the invention "could be detrimental to national security," it recommends to the Commissioner for Patents to issue a secrecy order. The Commissioner for Patents then issues this order.

Thus, the decision is made by a group of experts from various federal agencies who specialize in national security issues.

Could this be a "conflict of interest disguised as a threat"?

This is one of the most pressing questions concerning the Invention Secrecy Act. Critics and human rights activists express concerns about the possibility of a "conflict of interest" or abuse of power:

  • Lack of transparency: The classification process is very opaque. The inventor does not have access to the information on which the classification decision is based and cannot understand what exactly in his invention was considered a threat.
  • Economic espionage/Suppression of competition: Although the official purpose of patent classification is to protect national security, there is an opinion (although difficult to prove due to secrecy) that it can be used for:
    • Preventing the emergence of competitors: If a revolutionary technology can be developed by a private company but is not directly related to the state, its classification may prevent its commercialization, while the government or its related companies may develop similar technologies in secret.
    • Gaining government access to technology: Classification, especially if the invention is then used by the government, can force the inventor to seek compensation, effectively depriving him of the rights to commercial realization.
    • Covering up failures or delays in government projects: Classifying a similar private invention can hide the lagging behind of government programs.

Although direct evidence that secrecy orders are systematically used to mask conflicts of interest or industrial espionage is difficult to find due to the secrecy of the process itself, the potential for such abuse exists and causes concern.

Appeal procedure (appeal)?

Yes, a procedure for appealing a secrecy order exists, although it is extremely limited and difficult for an ordinary inventor:

  • Petition to the USPTO: The owner of a patent application on which a secrecy order has been imposed may file a petition with the Commissioner for Patents at the USPTO to request the cancellation or modification of the order.
    • To do this, it is necessary to provide a "complete statement of all facts and circumstances" on which the petition is based.
    • This petition will be forwarded to the very agency that initiated the secrecy order for consideration and a recommendation.
    • Based on this recommendation, the Commissioner for Patents makes the final decision. A common reason for revoking an order can be, for example, proof that the information has already been publicly disclosed (e.g., in a foreign publication).
  • Appeal to the Secretary of Commerce: If the Commissioner for Patents denies the petition for cancellation, the applicant has the right to file an appeal with the US Secretary of Commerce.
  • Lawsuit for compensation in the Court of Federal Claims: The Invention Secrecy Act provides that an inventor whose patent has been classified has the right to receive "reasonable compensation" from the government if he has suffered damage as a result of the order or if the government has used his invention. This lawsuit is filed in the Court of Federal Claims.
    • This is not a direct appeal of the secrecy order itself (i.e., it does not cancel the secrecy), but it is a way to get compensation for the damage caused by this order or for the government's use of the invention.
    • However, the process of proving damage and determining "reasonable compensation" can be very complex and lengthy.

Limitations and complexities of the appeal procedure:

  • Secrecy: The biggest problem is that all proceedings related to a secrecy order are confidential. The inventor does not know what specific information in his invention was recognized as a threat, which makes an reasoned challenge virtually impossible.
  • Lack of transparent judicial oversight: Direct judicial review of the validity of the secrecy order itself is extremely difficult or impossible in ordinary courts. Courts cannot easily access the classified information on which the decision is based.
  • "Internal" nature of the appeal: The first two stages of appeal (USPTO and the Secretary of Commerce) are essentially administrative appeals within the executive branch of government, and not a fully independent judicial review.
  • Burden of proof: The burden of proving that the order should be canceled lies with the inventor, who at the same time is deprived of all the necessary information.

Thus, although formal appeal mechanisms exist, they provide limited opportunities for the inventor to effectively fight a secrecy order. The system is heavily biased towards national security interests, as defined by the government.

    • Law: German Patent Law (Patentgesetz).

  • China:
    • Law: Chinese Patent Law.
    • How it works: China, like many other countries, has tightened its patent laws to include more comprehensive secrecy provisions, reflecting the growing importance of protecting technologies in national security strategies.   • India:
    • Law: Patents Act, 1970.

Common features of such laws:

  • Purpose: The main purpose of these laws is to protect national security, to prevent the transfer of critically important technologies to potential adversaries.
  • Mechanism: This usually involves checking patent applications for their potential military or strategic application. If such an application is found, a secrecy order can be issued that prohibits the publication and/or commercialization of the invention.
  • Scope of application: This often applies to technologies related to the defense industry, nuclear technologies, cryptography, advanced materials, bioengineering, and other areas that have a dual purpose (civilian and military).
  • Inventor's rights: In some cases, inventors may be provided with compensation for the restrictions imposed on their invention.

Thus, the United States is not unique in having this kind of legislation. Many countries, especially those that are actively developing advanced technologies and have serious national security interests, use similar mechanisms to control potentially dangerous inventions.
