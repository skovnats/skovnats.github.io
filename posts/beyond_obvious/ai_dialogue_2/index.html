💬 Question:

What is the term "golden billion" and what is its origin?

Please clarify: What meaning is attributed to this term? What ideas or theories are associated with it?

🤖 AI Response:

The term "golden billion" is a concept mainly used in Russian-language journalism, conspiracy theories, and geopolitical discussions. It refers to the population of the most developed and wealthy countries in the world, which, according to this concept, consumes the lion's share of global resources and seeks to maintain its privileged position at the expense of the rest of humanity.

Origin of the term:

The exact authorship of the term is difficult to establish, but it gained widespread use in Russia in the 1990s, especially after the collapse of the USSR. Its emergence is linked to understanding global inequality, sustainable development problems, and the role of the West in the global economy.

  • Anatoly Tsikunov (pseudonym A. Kuzmich): Often mentioned as one of the first authors who actively used and popularized this term in his works in the early 1990s. He developed the idea that wealthy Western countries strive to ensure a high standard of living for their population (the "golden billion") at the cost of exploiting the resources of the rest of the world.
  • Influence of Club of Rome ideas: The "golden billion" concept echoes some ideas expressed in Club of Rome reports (for example, "The Limits to Growth," 1972), which raised questions about the limitations of global resources and risks of unlimited consumption growth. However, the Club of Rome itself did not use the term "golden billion."
  • Post-Soviet context: In 1990s Russia, the term found fertile ground against the backdrop of economic difficulties, feelings of injustice in the world order, and the search for explanations for the country's changed role on the international stage.

Meaning of the term:

The main idea associated with the "golden billion" is as follows:

  • Privileged minority: The "golden billion" is a conventional designation for the population of the wealthiest countries (predominantly Western countries such as the USA, Canada, EU countries, Japan, Australia, and New Zealand). Their total population is approximately estimated at one billion people.
  • Disproportionate consumption: It is claimed that this "billion" consumes the overwhelming majority of global resources (energy, raw materials, food) and produces most waste and pollution, while the rest of the planet's population (several billion people) lives in much worse conditions.
  • Preserving the status quo: Supporters of the theory believe that the "golden billion" consciously or unconsciously strives to maintain this state of affairs, using economic, political, and even military levers to maintain their dominance and limit the development of other countries. This may include control over international financial institutions, imposing certain development models, etc.
  • Conspiratorial overtone: The term is often used in the context of conspiracy theories, suggesting the existence of some plan or secret conspiracy of "golden billion" elites to exploit the rest of the world, reduce its population, or prevent its economic growth.
  • Criticism of globalization and neocolonialism: The term is also used to criticize negative aspects of globalization and policies perceived as neocolonial by developed countries.

It's important to note that the term "golden billion" is more of a journalistic and ideological cliché than a strictly scientific concept. It is often used to express criticism of the existing world economic and political order and to denote the problem of global inequality.

💬 Question:

Is there a law in the USA that allows classifying patents if they pose a threat or are of interest to the state?

  • When was this law adopted?
  • How many patents have been classified under this law?

🤖 AI Response:

Yes, this is true. The United States has a law that allows the government to classify patent applications and even already issued patents if it is believed that their disclosure could harm national security.

Invention Secrecy Act of 1951

This law is called the Invention Secrecy Act of 1951, codified as 35 U.S. Code § 181.

When was the Invention Secrecy Act adopted?

The history of this law dates back to 1917, during World War I, when the first similar act was passed. However, its current version, the "Invention Secrecy Act," was adopted in 1951 at the height of the Cold War.

How does it work?

When a patent application is submitted to the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO), it undergoes initial review. If the invention concerns technologies that potentially could have military or strategic significance (for example, nuclear energy, defense systems, cryptography, new materials, or communication methods), the USPTO may refer it for review to appropriate defense or intelligence agencies (for example, Department of Defense, NSA).

If these agencies conclude that publication or disclosure of the invention (through application publication or patent issuance) "could be detrimental to national security," the Patent Commissioner may issue a "Secrecy Order."

This order:

  • Prohibits publication of the patent application.
  • Delays patent issuance.
  • Restricts disclosure of the invention by the inventor (prohibits sale, export, or discussion of the invention without permission).
  • Requires storage of documents related to the invention under secrecy conditions.
  • Usually renewed annually, unless there is a declared national emergency or war, when it remains in effect for the duration of the emergency plus six months.

It's important to note that such an order can be imposed even on inventions developed by private individuals or companies without any government funding or involvement.

How many patents are classified?

The exact number of classified patents is not publicly available information, as this itself is classified. However, the Federation of American Scientists (FAS), which deals with government secrecy issues, regularly publishes data obtained from the USPTO.

According to their reports:

  • At the end of fiscal year 2017 (September 30, 2017), there were 5,784 secrecy orders in effect in the USA.
  • At the end of fiscal year 2018 (September 30, 2018), this figure slightly increased to 5,792 orders. This was the highest figure since 1993.

These figures show that the practice of classifying patents not only exists but is actively used, and the number of such orders remains significant. Dozens of new secrecy orders are issued annually, while fewer old ones are rescinded.

Thus, the "Invention Secrecy Act of 1951" is a powerful tool in the hands of the US government to control the spread of technologies that may pose a threat to national security, and it is actively applied to thousands of inventions.

💬 Question:

Who determines that a patent poses a threat to US national security for its classification?

  • Could this be a conflict of interest disguised as a threat?
  • Is there a judicial procedure for appealing or challenging such decisions?

🤖 AI Response:

Indeed, the question of who makes the decision to classify a patent and whether there are mechanisms to challenge it is extremely important, especially considering potential "conflicts of interest."

Who decides it's a "threat"?

The decision to impose a secrecy order is not made by one person, but as a result of a process involving several federal agencies:

  • USPTO (United States Patent and Trademark Office): All patent applications submitted to the USPTO undergo initial review for sensitive information that may relate to national security. This review is conducted by specially appointed officials.
  • "Defense agencies": If potentially sensitive information is identified during initial review at the USPTO, the patent application is referred for consideration to one or more relevant "defense agencies." These agencies are designated by the President of the United States and include, but are not limited to:
    • Department of Defense (DoD)
    • National Security Agency (NSA)
    • Department of Energy (DoE), especially in matters related to nuclear technologies
    • NASA (National Aeronautics and Space Administration)
    • Department of Homeland Security
    • Department of Justice

    In general, any federal government agency that has the right to classify data can request patent classification.

  • Patent Commissioner: If one of these defense agencies concludes that publication or disclosure of the invention "could harm national security," it recommends that the Patent Commissioner issue a secrecy order. The Patent Commissioner then issues this order.

Thus, the decision is made by a group of experts from various federal agencies who specialize in national security matters.

Could this be a "conflict of interest disguised as a threat"?

This is one of the most acute questions regarding the Invention Secrecy Act. Critics and civil rights advocates express concerns about possible "conflicts of interest" or abuse of power:

  • Lack of transparency: The classification process is very opaque. The inventor has no access to information on which the classification decision is based and cannot understand what exactly in their invention was considered a threat.
  • Economic espionage/Competition suppression: Although officially patent classification is intended to protect national security, there is an opinion (though difficult to prove due to secrecy) that it could be used to:
    • Prevent competitors from emerging: If revolutionary technology could be developed by a private company, but it's not directly connected to the government, its classification could prevent its commercialization, while the government or government-related companies could develop similar technologies in secrecy.
    • Give government access to technology: Classification, especially if the invention is then used by the government, could force the inventor to seek compensation, effectively depriving them of rights to commercial implementation.
    • Cover failures or delays in government projects: Classifying a similar private invention could hide government program delays.

While direct evidence that secrecy orders are systematically used to mask conflicts of interest or industrial espionage is difficult to find due to the secrecy of the process itself, the potential for such abuse exists and raises concerns.

Challenge procedure (appeal)?

Yes, a procedure for challenging a secrecy order exists, although it is extremely limited and difficult for an ordinary inventor:

  • Petition to USPTO: The owner of a patent application subject to a secrecy order may file a petition to the Patent Commissioner at USPTO requesting rescission or modification of the order.
    • This requires providing "a complete statement of all facts and circumstances" on which the petition is based.
    • This petition will be referred to the same agency that initiated the secrecy order for consideration and recommendation.
    • Based on this recommendation, the Patent Commissioner makes a final decision. A common reason for rescinding an order might be, for example, proof that the information has already been publicly disclosed (e.g., in a foreign publication).
  • Appeal to the Secretary of Commerce: If the Patent Commissioner denies the petition for rescission, the applicant has the right to appeal to the US Secretary of Commerce.
  • Compensation lawsuit in the Court of Federal Claims: The Invention Secrecy Act provides that an inventor whose patent has been classified has the right to receive "reasonable compensation" from the government if they suffered damage as a result of the order or if the government used their invention. This lawsuit is filed in the Court of Federal Claims.
    • This is not a direct appeal of the secrecy order itself (i.e., it does not remove the secrecy), but it is a way to receive reimbursement for damage caused by this order or for government use of the invention.
    • However, the process of proving damage and determining "reasonable compensation" can be very complex and lengthy.

Limitations and complexities of the challenge procedure:

  • Secrecy: The biggest problem is that all proceedings related to the secrecy order are classified. The inventor doesn't know what specific information in their invention was deemed a threat, making it practically impossible to make an argued challenge.
  • Lack of transparent judicial oversight: Direct judicial review of the validity of the secrecy order itself is extremely difficult or impossible in ordinary courts. Courts cannot easily access classified information on which the decision is based.
  • "Internal" nature of appeal: The first two levels of appeal (USPTO and Secretary of Commerce) are essentially administrative appeals within the executive branch, not fully independent judicial review.
  • Burden of proof: The burden of proving that the order should be rescinded lies with the inventor, who is deprived of all necessary information.

Thus, while formal challenge mechanisms exist, they provide limited opportunities for an inventor to effectively fight a secrecy order. The system is heavily tilted toward national security interests as defined by the government.

💬 Question:

In which countries do laws exist that allow classifying or effectively banning patents on grounds of state security?

  • Which countries apply similar measures to restrict patents?
  • What are the mechanisms for classifying patents in these countries?
  • Are there examples of such restrictions related to national security?

🤖 AI Response:

Yes, similar laws allowing "classification" of patent applications or inventions if they pose a threat to national security exist not only in the USA but also in many other countries worldwide. This practice is not new and dates back to World War I and II, when governments realized the need to control new technologies with military applications.

Countries with similar patent classification mechanisms:

  • United Kingdom:
    • Law: Invention secrecy provisions are contained in the Patents Act 1977.
    • How it works: The Controller of the UK Intellectual Property Office may issue a "Secrecy Direction" if publication of a patent application or patent grant could harm national security or public order. This may concern both defense technologies and, for example, nuclear energy-related technologies.
  • Germany:
    • Law: German Patent Law (Patentgesetz).
    • How it works: The German Patent and Trade Mark Office (DPMA) also has authority to classify inventions. An application may be classified if its disclosure could threaten state security. This may concern both defense and other strategically important technologies.
  • France:
    • Law: French Intellectual Property Code.
    • How it works: French legislation also provides for the possibility of imposing restrictions on publication of patent applications for national defense reasons.
  • Japan:
    • Law: Japanese Patent Act.
    • How it works: Japan also has provisions allowing the Japan Patent Office (JPO) Commissioner to issue a secrecy order if an invention could undermine the country's and its citizens' security through external actions. This is especially relevant for encryption-related technologies or other sensitive areas.
  • China:
    • Law: PRC Patent Law.
    • How it works: China, like many other countries, has tightened its patent laws to include more comprehensive secrecy provisions, reflecting the growing importance of technology protection in national security strategies. The China National Intellectual Property Administration (CNIPA) has authority to classify inventions.
  • India:
    • Law: Patents Act, 1970.
    • How it works: In India, if an invention may have potential significance for national security, the Controller General of Patents may issue a "secrecy direction."
  • Russia:
    • Law: Civil Code of the Russian Federation (Part 4, Article 1395).
    • How it works: Russian legislation requires that applications for inventions or utility models created in the Russian Federation first be filed with Rospatent (Federal Service for Intellectual Property). Only after six months (or after special examination at the applicant's request) and provided the application contains no state secret information, can an application be filed in a foreign state. This essentially allows Rospatent or other competent authorities to identify and, if necessary, classify inventions constituting state secrets before they become available abroad.

Common features of such laws:

  • Purpose: The main purpose of these laws is to protect national security and prevent transfer of critically important technologies to potential adversaries.
  • Mechanism: Usually involves reviewing patent applications for potential military or strategic applications. If such application is found, a secrecy order may be issued prohibiting publication and/or commercialization of the invention.
  • Scope: Often concerns technologies related to defense industry, nuclear technologies, cryptography, advanced materials, bioengineering, and other dual-use areas (civilian and military).
  • Inventor rights: In some cases, inventors may be provided compensation for restrictions imposed on their invention.

Thus, the USA is not unique in having such legislation. Many countries, especially those actively developing advanced technologies and having serious national security interests, use similar mechanisms to control potentially dangerous inventions.

💬 Question:

Could an invention in the USA of fuel that is significantly cheaper and more convenient than gasoline be considered a threat to national security?

  • What consequences, such as billions in losses for oil companies, loss of market control, and reduced role of the dollar in oil trade, could affect the economy?
  • How could such fuel innovations change global energy policy?

🤖 AI Response:

Certainly, an invention of fuel that is significantly cheaper and more convenient than traditional fuel, capable of completely displacing gasoline, would very likely be considered a threat to US national security, despite the apparent "usefulness" for the global economy and environment.

Why is this a threat?

Economic stability and dollar dominance:

  • Petrodollar: The US dollar plays a central role in the global economy largely because oil (and many other commodities) is traded in dollars. This creates enormous demand for the dollar, supports its stability, and gives the USA significant geopolitical influence. If gasoline is displaced by another fuel, demand for oil will sharply decline, and with it, the position of the petrodollar will weaken.
  • Corporate losses: American and international oil companies that have invested trillions of dollars in extraction, refining, and transportation of oil and gas would suffer colossal losses. This could cause stock market collapse, mass bankruptcies, loss of millions of jobs, and trigger a global financial crisis starting in the USA.
  • Energy security: Although new fuel might be "cheaper and more convenient," its implementation would require complete reconstruction of the entire energy infrastructure. The transition period would be extremely unstable, and control over the new technology and its production would become a matter of survival. If the invention were made by a private individual or foreign power, this could threaten US energy independence.

Geopolitical influence and balance of power:

  • Loss of influence: The current geopolitical map is largely determined by dependence on oil and gas. The USA uses its control over global financial markets and military power to ensure stable oil supplies and protect its interests in oil-producing regions. Eliminating this dependence would radically change the balance of power, potentially weakening US influence and strengthening other powers that could quickly adapt to the new technology or gain control over it.
  • Military operations: The US Armed Forces significantly depend on petroleum fuels. Complete transition to new fuel would require decades of restructuring, creating vulnerabilities in the military sphere.

Domestic social and political stability:

  • Social unrest: Mass layoffs in the energy sector, corporate bankruptcies, and dramatic changes in the lifestyle of millions of people connected to the automotive and oil industries could lead to serious social unrest and political instability within the country.
  • Lobbying: The oil and gas industry is one of the most powerful lobbying forces in the USA. Its interests are closely intertwined with the political system, and any radical change threatening its existence would be met with colossal resistance.

Technology control:

  • If such an invention appeared with a private individual or company, the US government would strive to gain complete control over it to dictate the terms of its implementation. Patent classification (or even its forced purchase) would be a logical step to manage this process and minimize potential chaos.
  • There's a risk that the technology could fall into the hands of adversaries, giving them significant advantage.

Conclusion:

Yes, an invention of such "perfect" fuel capable of displacing gasoline would almost certainly be considered a serious threat to US national security. This is related not to the fuel itself, but to the catastrophic economic, geopolitical, and social consequences that an instant and uncontrolled transition would entail. The US government would likely take all possible measures, including patent classification, to control the implementation of such technology and ensure a managed transition, minimizing damage to the existing system and preserving its strategic interests.

💬 Question:

Wouldn't the invention of cheap and convenient fuel become a common human good, especially for countries that don't have access to oil due to US actions?

  • Could such fuel benefit humanity despite financial and other losses for influential groups in the USA?
  • How would such an invention affect global resource distribution and economic justice?

🤖 AI Response:

Yes, on one hand, your statement is absolutely correct: the invention of such "miracle fuel" could become an enormous benefit for all humanity, solving many problems:

  • Ecology: Significant reduction in greenhouse gas emissions if this fuel is clean, and reduction in air pollution.
  • Economic growth and poverty alleviation: Lower energy costs would dramatically reduce production costs, which could lead to lower prices for goods and services, stimulate economic growth in developing countries, and improve the quality of life for millions of people.
  • Geopolitical stability: Eliminating dependence on oil would potentially remove many sources of conflict related to control over energy resources and reduce the influence of authoritarian regimes that use oil as a tool of blackmail or enrichment.
  • Energy independence: Countries without their own oil reserves (such as Germany, Japan, South Korea, as well as many developing countries) would gain enormous advantages, becoming independent from imports.

Contradiction between "common human good" and "national security"

Here lies the key contradiction. What is a "good for all humanity" in the long term may be perceived as an immediate and catastrophic threat to the existing world order and national interests of specific influential players.

When the US government (or any other major power) assesses "threat to national security," it primarily considers:

Immediate consequences for their country:

  • Economic collapse: Instant devaluation of the oil and gas industry would lead to bankruptcy of major corporations, mass unemployment, stock price falls, bond defaults, and potentially to a global financial crisis starting in the USA and oil-exporting countries. No government wants such a scenario on its territory.
  • Social unrest: Economic destabilization could lead to mass protests, riots, and declining trust in government.
  • Loss of geopolitical influence: If the dollar ceases to be the main currency of the energy market, this would undermine US financial dominance and ability to influence global processes through economic levers.

Uncontrolled change in balance of power:

  • Governments, including the USA, are extremely sensitive to unpredictable changes in international politics. Immediate change in the energy landscape would lead to chaos in global diplomacy, trade, and military strategies. Oil-exporting countries that lose revenue could become unstable, creating new centers of tension.
  • Question of "who's first": Even if this is a "good," any major power would want to be the first to control this technology, to use it for their own benefit or at least to manage the transition period. Releasing it "into the world" without control means giving up initiative to anyone.

Why "forcefully took for themselves"?

Regarding your remark that the USA "forcefully took for themselves" access to oil and other resources: this is a common critical viewpoint. Historically and geopolitically, the USA has indeed played and continues to play a significant role in ensuring stable oil supplies, often intervening in the affairs of oil-producing regions (Middle East, Latin America). Many believe this was done in the interests of American corporations and to maintain the dollar's dominant role.

If this system suddenly collapses due to the emergence of new fuel, this would be perceived as a loss of strategic advantage and control that the USA has carefully built over decades. For those in power and responsible for "national interests" in their current understanding, this is a threat, not a benefit, at least in the short term.

Conclusion

Thus, an invention capable of bringing "common human good" in the long term may be perceived as an immediate and destructive threat to national security and existing interests of influential circles. Patent classification of such an invention under the Invention Secrecy Act would be aimed at buying time for the government to control the technology implementation process, minimize risks to its economy and geopolitical position, and possibly develop its own strategy for using this technology in its interests.

💬 Question:

How did the petrodollar arise and what economic and political benefits does it bring to the USA?

  • When and how did the petrodollar system form?
  • What economic advantages does it provide to the USA?
  • How does the petrodollar strengthen US political influence?

🤖 AI Response:

The concept of **"petrodollar"** describes a system where oil, the most traded commodity in the world, is sold and bought on global markets predominantly in US dollars. This became one of the pillars of American economic and political power after abandoning the gold standard.

How did the petrodollar emerge?

The origins of the petrodollar system lie in two key events of the 1970s:

Abandonment of the gold standard (1971):

Until 1971, the US dollar was pegged to gold at a fixed rate within the Bretton Woods system. However, by the early 1970s, the USA faced pressure due to growing budget deficits (related to the Vietnam War and domestic spending) and increasing amounts of dollars in circulation worldwide. This led to pressure on US gold reserves, as other countries began exchanging their dollars for gold. In August 1971, President Richard Nixon unilaterally ended dollar convertibility to gold, effectively ending the Bretton Woods system. This meant the dollar became a fiat currency (its value was not tied to a physical commodity but determined by trust and demand).

The 1973 oil crisis and agreement with Saudi Arabia:

  • In October 1973, the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries (OAPEC), in response to Western support for Israel in the Yom Kippur War, declared an oil embargo and sharply raised oil prices. This led to a global energy crisis.
  • During this period, the USA, seeking to stabilize the global economy and support dollar demand (which had lost its "gold" backing), concluded a series of agreements with the key player in the oil market – Saudi Arabia. Although there is no official "petrodollar pact" as a single signed document, this was a series of arrangements reached in 1973-1974, whose main elements were:
    • Oil sales for dollars: Saudi Arabia (and then most other OPEC countries) agreed to sell their oil exclusively in US dollars. This forced any country wanting to buy oil to first convert their national currency to US dollars, creating permanent global demand for the dollar.
    • "Petrodollar recycling": In exchange, the USA offered Saudi Arabia (and other producers) military protection and assistance in modernization, as well as guarantees that their huge oil revenues would be safely invested in the US financial system (mainly in US Treasury bonds).

Thus, the "petrodollar" emerged as a result of these mutually beneficial arrangements that secured the US dollar's role as the main reserve and trading currency in global energy.

Economic benefits for the USA:

The petrodollar system provides the USA with significant economic advantages:

  • Permanent global demand for US dollars:
    • Every country buying oil (which is practically all countries) is forced to hold significant reserves of US dollars. This creates permanent and enormous demand for the American currency, supporting its high value and stability.
    • This allows the USA to "print" dollars for their needs (for example, to cover budget deficits) without the strong inflation that would occur without this external demand.
  • Low interest rates and easy debt financing:
    • Oil-exporting countries, receiving huge dollar revenues, often reinvest them in American assets, especially US government Treasury bonds. This process is called "petrodollar recycling."
    • This constant capital inflow to the USA creates high demand for American bonds, allowing the US government to borrow money at lower interest rates. This significantly reduces the cost of financing US government debt.
  • Reserve currency status:
    • The dollar's role as the main currency for oil trade reinforced its status as the primary global reserve currency. This means central banks worldwide prefer to hold their reserves in US dollars.
    • This status gives the USA enormous financial flexibility, including the ability to more easily respond to economic crises and maintain low inflation.
  • Strengthening American financial markets:
    • Investments by oil-producing countries in the USA contribute to the growth of American stock markets, real estate markets, and other sectors, increasing their liquidity.

Political benefits for the USA:

Besides economic benefits, the petrodollar gives the USA significant political power and influence:

  • Geopolitical influence:
    • The USA gains significant influence over oil-exporting countries (especially in the Middle East), offering them military protection and stability in exchange for supporting the petrodollar system.
    • The ability to "disconnect" countries from the dollar financial system (for example, through sanctions) becomes a powerful foreign policy tool, as this makes it difficult for them to buy and sell oil, and thus participate in global trade.
  • Energy security:
    • Although the USA is itself a major oil producer, the petrodollar system contributes to global oil price stability (since everything is tied to one currency) and ensures constant access to global supplies, which is critically important for the economy and security.
  • Global dominance:
    • The dollar as the dominant currency in energy trade is a symbol and tool of global economic and political dominance by the USA. This allows the USA to largely shape the rules of international trade and finance.

Current relevance:

In recent years, the petrodollar's role has been under increasing pressure. Some countries, such as Russia and China, are actively promoting the idea of dedollarization, seeking to conduct oil and other commodity trade in their national currencies or other currencies (for example, yuan). Reports about the expiration of the 50-year agreement between the USA and Saudi Arabia (although its nature is not always interpreted similarly by different sources) also raise discussions about the future of the petrodollar. Nevertheless, despite these challenges, the US dollar remains the dominant currency in global oil trade and global finance.

💬 Question:

Why was the gold standard abolished and who benefits from this?

  • What reasons led to abandoning the gold standard?
  • Which groups or countries gained economic and political benefits from this abolition?
  • How did abandoning the gold standard affect the global economy?

🤖 AI Response:

The departure from the gold standard was one of the most significant changes in world economic history. This process occurred gradually, but the final abandonment by most countries, including the USA, happened in the 20th century.

Why abandon the gold standard?

The main reasons for abandoning the gold standard can be reduced to its inflexibility and limitations for managing modern economy:

Limited money supply:

  • Gold binding: Under the gold standard, the amount of money in circulation (money supply) directly depended on a country's gold reserves. To print new money required either mining more gold or importing it.
  • Mismatch with economic growth: Countries' economies grew much faster than global gold production. This created deflationary pressure, where each dollar/pound/mark became more valuable, but this also meant that lending and investment were limited due to insufficient money to finance the expanding economy. Deflation makes debt repayment more difficult, which can slow economic activity.
  • Lack of flexibility in crisis: During economic downturns (recessions) or financial crises, governments and central banks could not simply "print" more money to stimulate the economy, reduce unemployment, or save banks. Their hands were tied by the amount of gold. This exacerbated crises, making them longer and more painful.

Vulnerability to "gold runs":

  • During instability (political or economic), people could massively try to exchange their paper money for gold, fearing currency devaluation. This led to "gold" panics, depletion of central bank gold reserves, and consequently, financial system collapse.
  • Britain, for example, was forced to finally abandon the gold standard in 1931 during the Great Depression due to massive capital "flight" and gold reserve depletion.

Influence of wars:

  • War financing: Wars require enormous expenditures. Under the gold standard, governments would find it extremely difficult to finance large-scale military actions without significantly raising taxes or foreign loans, as they couldn't simply print more money. During World War I, many countries temporarily or permanently abandoned the gold standard to be able to print money for financing military efforts.
  • Post-war inflation: After wars, when countries returned to the gold standard, they had to fight inflation caused by money printing during wartime, which often led to painful deflationary periods.

Gold price volatility:

  • Gold market prices can change due to discovery of new deposits, changes in demand (for example, in jewelry industry), or political instability. These fluctuations affect the value of gold-pegged currency and can create instability in the economy.

Lack of control over monetary policy:

  • Central banks (such as the US Federal Reserve or Bank of England) did not have full freedom in conducting monetary policy to achieve goals such as maintaining full employment or price stability. Their decisions were tied to gold reserves, limiting their ability to respond to economic cycles.

Who benefits from abandoning the gold standard (transition to fiat money)?

The departure from the gold standard and transition to fiat money (money whose value is not tied to a physical commodity but determined by trust in the government and its willingness to accept them as tax payment) was beneficial primarily to governments and central banks, as well as the economy as a whole (in theory).

Benefits for governments and central banks:

Monetary policy flexibility:

  • Fighting recessions and unemployment: Central banks can increase or decrease money supply, lower or raise interest rates to stimulate the economy during downturns or contain inflation during overheating. This allows smoothing economic cycles and avoiding deep depressions.
  • Financing government spending: Governments can more easily finance large projects, social programs, or military spending by issuing government bonds that the central bank can purchase, effectively "printing" money (though this carries inflation risk).

Managing inflation (and deflation):

  • Under a fiat currency, the central bank can more actively manage inflation to keep it at a low, stable level. Slight inflation is considered beneficial for stimulating spending and investment. The gold standard, conversely, often led to deflation.

Avoiding "gold runs":

  • Since there's no possibility to exchange money for a physical commodity, the threat of gold reserve depletion disappears.

Strengthening sovereignty:

  • Abandoning the gold standard strengthens a country's financial sovereignty, as its monetary policy no longer depends on gold price fluctuations or other countries' gold reserves.

Benefits for the economy as a whole:

Faster economic growth:

  • The ability to expand money supply as the economy grows and credit availability for investment contributes to faster and more stable economic growth.
  • The fiat system allows banks to operate with fractional reserves, which multiplies money supply and stimulates lending and investment.

Price stability (ideally):

  • Although the fiat system is not immune to inflation, competent central bank management can provide more stable price levels in the long term than a system tied to a volatile commodity.

International trade:

  • Flexible exchange rates (not tied to gold) allow countries to adjust their economies and maintain competitiveness in international trade.

Examples:

  • Great Depression: Many economists believe that countries that abandoned the gold standard faster during the Great Depression (for example, Britain) were able to recover from the crisis faster, as their central banks gained the ability to conduct stimulative monetary policy.
  • USA in 1971: Abandoning the gold peg allowed the USA to remove restrictions on money supply and more freely finance their domestic programs and military operations.

Overall, the transition from the gold standard to fiat money was a pragmatic decision that provided governments and central banks with unprecedented control over the economy, allowing them to actively manage inflation, unemployment, and economic growth, which was impossible under strict gold binding. However, this also placed enormous responsibility on them, as irresponsible monetary policy can lead to hyperinflation and loss of currency confidence.
