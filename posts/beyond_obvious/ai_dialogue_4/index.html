2. Do revolutions affect international obligations?

The short answer: usually no.

In international law, the principle of state continuity prevails. This principle states that a change of government or even political system (including as a result of a revolution, coup, or civil war) does not terminate the existence of the state itself as a subject of international law.

This means that:

    ¬† ¬†
  • All international treaties signed and ratified by the previous legitimate government remain binding on the new government.
    • ¬† ¬†
  • The state continues to be responsible for its international obligations (e.g., debts).
    • ¬† ¬†
  • A new government cannot simply state: "We do not recognize the treaties signed by our predecessors." Unilateral non-compliance with a treaty is a violation of international law.

Exceptions and nuances:

    ¬† ¬†
  • **"Odious debts":** There is a doctrine according to which debts taken by a dictatorial regime not for the benefit of the nation but to suppress the people may not be recognized by the new government. However, this doctrine is controversial and rarely applied in practice.
    • ¬† ¬†
  • **Disintegration of the state:** If a state ceases to exist and new ones arise in its place (as in the case of the USSR or Yugoslavia), the issue of succession to treaties is resolved separately and is complex.

Thus, from the point of view of international law, the change of power in Kyiv in 2014 did not annul Ukraine's international obligations assumed by previous governments.
