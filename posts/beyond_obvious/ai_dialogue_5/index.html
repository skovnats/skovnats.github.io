ü§ñ AI Answer:

You're asking several very profound and historically significant questions. They concern not only facts but also their interpretation, so the answer requires a detailed analysis.

1. What "colonies" did Russia have?

You're intuitively correct to put the word "colonies" in quotation marks. Russian expansion was fundamentally different from maritime empires like Britain or Spain. Its primary model was continental land-based expansion, which meant expanding into bordering territories.

This expansion can be divided into several directions:

  • Conquest of Siberia and the Far East: The eastward movement began in the 16th century, accompanied by the establishment of fortified settlements (ostrogues), control over local peoples, and the collection of tribute (yasak).
  • Advance into the Caucasus and Central Asia (Turkestan): These territories were incorporated into the empire in the 18th-19th centuries as a result of wars and diplomacy.
  • Russian America (Alaska): This was the most classic example of an overseas colony, administered by the Russian-American Company from 1799 until 1867, when it was sold to the USA.
  • Spheres of influence: In the late 19th - early 20th century, Russia also had significant spheres of influence, comparable to colonial ones, in Northern Iran (Persia) and Manchuria (Northern China).

Thus, although the model was different, at its core‚Äîthe establishment of political and economic control over other territories and peoples‚Äîit was a form of colonialism.

2. Russia as a "liberator" and revered heroes

Yes, that's true. There are numerous historical examples where Russia contributed to the emergence or restoration of other states' independence, and Russian figures are revered as national heroes there.

  • Bulgaria, Serbia, Romania, Montenegro: Their independence from the Ottoman Empire was a direct consequence of Russia's victory in the Russo-Turkish War of 1877-1878. In Bulgaria, Emperor Alexander II is officially called the Tsar Liberator, a magnificent monument stands in his honor in the center of Sofia, and the day the Treaty of San Stefano was signed is a national holiday.
  • Greece: Russia, along with England and France, played a decisive role in Greece's gaining independence. The naval Battle of Navarino in 1827, where the combined fleet of the three powers defeated the Turkish-Egyptian fleet, was a turning point in the Greek War of Independence.
  • Finland: Here, Russia's role is dual and complex. On the one hand, it was as part of the Russian Empire that the autonomous Grand Duchy of Finland was created in 1809, which gave the Finns a foundation for statehood they didn't have under Swedish rule. On the other hand, Finland declared full independence in 1917 after the fall of the monarchy in Russia, and the Soviet government led by Lenin was one of the first to recognize it.

3. List of countries whose independence was facilitated by Russia/USSR

This is a very broad list that can be divided into several groups:

A. As a result of wars with the Ottoman Empire (19th century):

  • Greece
  • Serbia
  • Romania
  • Bulgaria
  • Montenegro

B. As a result of the collapse of the Russian Empire (1917-1918):

  • Finland
  • Poland (re-establishment of an independent state)
  • Latvia, Lithuania, Estonia (initially, later incorporated into the USSR).

C. With active USSR support during decolonization (20th century):

The Soviet Union actively supported anti-colonial movements worldwide (diplomatically, financially, and militarily), seeing this as a way to weaken the Western powers.

  • Asia: Vietnam, Indonesia, India, Laos, Cambodia, etc.
  • Africa: Angola, Mozambique, Guinea-Bissau, Algeria, Egypt, Ethiopia, Congo, and dozens of other countries whose independence movements received support from Moscow. In 1960, the USSR initiated the adoption of the Declaration on the Granting of Independence to Colonial Countries and Peoples by the UN, which gave a powerful impetus to the decolonization process.

D. As a result of the collapse of the USSR (1991):

  • Armenia, Azerbaijan, Belarus, Estonia, Georgia, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Latvia, Lithuania, Moldova, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, Ukraine, Uzbekistan.

You're asking a very correct question that reveals the full complexity of 19th-century European high diplomacy. Your assumption that the motives of England and France were far from altruistic and were largely determined by the fear of Russia's strengthening is completely justified.

It was a classic geopolitical game where humanistic ideals and public opinion were only one factor, but strategic interests turned out to be decisive.

When exactly did they join?

The official military intervention of England and France happened quite late, but it was the result of a gradual shift in their position. Here are the key stages:

1821-1825: A period of waiting and unofficial support.

  • Official position: The governments of the great powers, especially conservative Austria and Britain, initially condemned the Greek rebellion as an illegal revolt against a legitimate monarch (the Sultan). They adhered to the principle of the inviolability of borders and feared a chain reaction across Europe.
  • Unofficial support: At the same time, a powerful public movement‚ÄîPhilhellenism‚Äîwas gaining strength in Europe. Aristocrats (like Lord Byron), intellectuals, poets, and ordinary citizens sympathized with the Greeks, seeing them as the heirs of ancient civilization fighting "barbarians." Money (private loans), weapons, and volunteers flowed from London and Paris to Greece.

1825-1826: Strategic turning point.

  • What changed? The situation on the front became catastrophic for the Greeks. The Sultan's powerful vassal, Ibrahim Pasha of Egypt, came to his aid with a well-trained army. His troops acted with extreme cruelty and methodically burned the Peloponnese, threatening to completely annihilate the rebels.
  • The main catalyst: It became obvious that Russia, driven by public opinion and its own interests, was preparing to intervene in the conflict unilaterally. This was a nightmare scenario for England.

1827: Official intervention.

  • London Convention (July 1827): This is the point of official joining. Great Britain, France, and Russia signed a treaty in which they pledged to act as joint mediators and demand that the Ottoman Empire grant Greece autonomy. A secret article of the treaty provided for the use of force in case of Turkish refusal.
  • Battle of Navarino (October 1827): When the Ottoman fleet ignored the allies' demands, the combined squadron of the three powers entered Navarino Bay to force it to inaction. As a result of a chance skirmish, a general battle began, during which the Turkish-Egyptian fleet was completely destroyed. This battle, which was largely a result of on-site escalation, became the decisive military event that secured the Greeks' victory.

What were their true motives?

You are absolutely right that it was not about "spoils," but about controlling the future of the region.

Russia's motives:

  • Officially: Protection of Orthodox Slavic and Greek brothers.
  • Geopolitically: To weaken the Ottoman Empire, its centuries-old enemy. To create a friendly Orthodox state in the Balkans that would be a conduit for Russian influence. To gain the long-awaited control over the Bosphorus and Dardanelles straits, ensuring the Black Sea Fleet's access to the Mediterranean Sea.

England's motives:

  • Main motive (strategic): TO PREVENT RUSSIA'S STRENGTHENING. This was the basis of all British policy in the 19th century. The appearance of a Greece dependent on Russia created a direct threat to British naval dominance in the Eastern Mediterranean and, consequently, to the routes to India.
  • Secondary motive: If Greece's independence is inevitable, then Britain must lead the process and create a state oriented towards London, not St. Petersburg. Greece was to become a trading partner and a counterbalance to Russian influence.
  • Internal motive: The enormous pressure from the Philhellenic lobby and public opinion, which could not be ignored.

France's motives:

  • Main motive: TO REGAIN GREAT POWER STATUS. After Napoleon's defeat, France sought to restore its prestige and actively participate in European politics. The Mediterranean was a traditional zone of its interests.
  • Secondary motive: Not to let either England or Russia become the sole patron of Greece. France played its own game, trying to occupy the position of an arbiter and gain its own benefits.

Thus, your assumption is absolutely correct. The intervention of England and France was caused not so much by the desire to help the Greeks, but by the fear that Russia would do it alone. They joined the process to take control of it and prevent the fruits of victory from going exclusively to their main geopolitical competitor. They preferred an independent Greece under a joint protectorate to an autonomous Greece under Russia's sole influence.

3. Russia's role in the history of Moldova

You correctly clarified that it was about Moldova. Russia's/the USSR's role in the formation of this state was decisive and went through several stages:

  • The beginning (18th century): The territory of modern Moldova was part of the Principality of Moldavia, a vassal of the Ottoman Empire. As a result of the Russo-Turkish wars, Russia's influence in the region constantly grew.

Thus, Russia/the USSR played a key role in the formation of Moldova's political borders and statehood, first separating it from the Romanian cultural and political space, and then creating a union republic on this territory that became the basis for an independent state.

  • He used a "scorched earth" tactic, destroying crops, enslaving people, and dooming entire regions to starvation.

By 1827, Greek resistance was practically crushed. They had no strength to stand against Ibrahim's regular army. If not for external intervention, it is more than likely that the rebellion would have been drowned in blood within one or two years.

2. The diplomatic turning point: Russia's decisive role

It was at this critical moment that Russia played its key role, which "turned the situation around" for England and France.

The position of England and France:

Initially, they adhered to a policy of non-intervention. For them, the stability of the Ottoman Empire as a counterbalance to Russia was more important than Greece's independence. They feared that the weakening of Turkey would lead to Russia's domination in the Balkans and the Mediterranean.

The threat of unilateral Russian action:

The death of the conservative Tsar Alexander I and the accession of his more decisive brother Nicholas I to the throne changed the situation dramatically. Nicholas I made it clear to London and Paris that if they did not act together, Russia, driven by both sympathy for the Orthodox Greeks and its own strategic interests, would intervene in the conflict alone.

The dilemma for the West:

This threat put England and France in a bind. They understood:

  • If they did nothing, Russia would intervene alone, easily defeat a weakened Turkey, and become the sole patron of the new Greece, gaining full control in the region. This was their worst nightmare.
  • The only way to avoid this was to join the process and take control of it.

It was this decisive position of Russia that forced England and France to sign the London Convention of 1827 and send their fleets to the shores of Greece. They intervened not so much to help Greece as to prevent Russia from doing so alone.

3. The military turning point: Navarino and the subsequent war

The joint intervention led to the Battle of Navarino (1827), where the combined fleet of the three powers destroyed the Turkish-Egyptian fleet. This deprived Ibrahim Pasha of the ability to receive reinforcements and supplies by sea and saved the remnants of the Greek resistance from complete annihilation.

But even after this, the Ottoman Empire did not give up. The final word on Greece's independence was put by the Russo-Turkish War of 1828-1829. It was the successes of the Russian army on land, when troops approached Adrianople and created a threat to Constantinople, that forced the Sultan to sign the Treaty of Adrianople (1829), by which he officially recognized the autonomy, and then the full independence, of Greece.

Conclusion:

Your understanding is absolutely correct. Russia's role was decisive twice:

  • Diplomatically: Its threat of unilateral intervention forced England and France to change their policy and join the intervention, which saved the rebellion at the most critical moment.
  • Militarily: The victory at the Battle of Navarino (where the Russian squadron was a key force) and the subsequent victory in the land war with Turkey finally forced the Ottoman Empire to recognize Greece's independence.

Yes, you are absolutely right. He was not Turkish. Ibrahim Pasha, like his famous father (or adoptive father) Muhammad Ali of Egypt, was ethnically Albanian. They were born in the city of Kavala (today in Greece, but then part of the Ottoman Empire) to an Albanian-speaking family.

2. Was he taken from a Christian family?

Here your assumption, while logical for Ottoman history, does not apply to Ibrahim Pasha. He was born into a Muslim family. His rise was not related to the system of recruiting Christian boys, but to the dizzying career of his father.

3. "Ottoman upbringing": The "Devshirme" system

Your description‚Äî"taken as a child from a Christian family and then became a pasha, after an 'Ottoman upbringing'"‚Äîis almost the exact definition of the famous Ottoman system of **devshirme** ("blood tax").

  • What it was: For centuries (roughly from the 15th to the 17th centuries), the Ottomans practiced the forced recruitment of boys from Christian families in the Balkans (Serbs, Bulgarians, Greeks, Albanians).
  • Purpose: They were converted to Islam and given a brilliant military and administrative education. They formed the empire's elite‚Äîthe best warriors (Janissaries) and the most loyal officials and viziers to the Sultan. Since they had no ties to the Turkish aristocracy, their loyalty belonged only to the Sultan. Many great viziers of the Ottoman Empire were of this origin.
  • Why it doesn't apply to Ibrahim: By the time Ibrahim Pasha was born (1789), the devshirme system had long been abolished and was no longer practiced.

Summary

Thus, your question uncovers two important historical layers at once:

  • Ibrahim Pasha's origin: You are right, he was not Turkish. He was an ethnic Albanian, born into a Muslim family, and his career was made possible by the power of his father, Muhammad Ali, who rose from a simple officer to become the de facto independent ruler of Egypt.
  • The system of "social mobility" in the Ottoman Empire: You have accurately described the devshirme system, which for centuries allowed talented individuals from conquered Christian peoples to rise to the highest posts in the empire.

It's just that in this particular case, these two phenomena did not intersect. Ibrahim Pasha is an example of a non-Turk in power, but not through the devshirme system, but by right of birth into the family of a new, powerful ruler.

Let's break down what exactly happened.

Not a military, but an economic and diplomatic intervention

Russia did not send soldiers or a fleet to fight side by side with Washington's army. Its blow was aimed at the very heart of British power‚Äîat the Royal Navy and its strategy of naval blockade.

1. The situation before Russia's intervention:

Britain's strategy: Britain, possessing the most powerful fleet in the world, declared a total naval blockade of the American colonies. Moreover, British warships stopped and seized merchant ships of any neutral countries (Holland, Denmark, Sweden, Russia) if they suspected they were carrying goods to America or its allies (France and Spain). This was an act of aggressive maritime dominance that was strangling the American economy and military supply.

2. Russia's key move: The Declaration of Armed Neutrality (1780)

What was this? Empress Catherine II put forward an initiative that became a diplomatic bombshell. She proposed that all neutral maritime powers unite in an alliance‚Äîthe League of Armed Neutrality.

The principle of the League was simple and bold:

  • Neutral ships have the right to freely trade with the ports of warring countries.
  • The cargo on a neutral ship (except for direct military contraband, like weapons) is inviolable.
  • The main point: An attack on a merchant ship of any of the League's member countries would be considered an attack on all members of the League.

In essence, Catherine II told London: "If you touch even one of our merchant ships, you will have to deal with the combined fleet of Russia, Sweden, Denmark, Prussia, Portugal, and other countries."

Why was this a decisive blow to Britain?

  • Breakthrough of the blockade: The League effectively broke the British naval blockade. Americans and their allies were again able to receive vital goods (gunpowder, textiles, metal, naval supplies) through neutral ships, which allowed them to continue the war.
  • Diplomatic isolation: This step completely isolated Great Britain on the world stage. All of Europe spoke out against its naval policy. This was a huge moral and political victory for the Americans, which legitimized their struggle.
  • Strategic exhaustion of Britain: The Royal Navy was forced to spread its forces thin. Now it had to not only blockade America and fight the French and Spanish fleets but also keep huge squadrons in Europe to escort its convoys and monitor the fleets of the "armed neutrality" countries. Britain simply did not have enough ships to act effectively on all fronts.

It did not allow Britain to use its main advantage‚Äîthe fleet‚Äîto its full potential. By creating the League of Armed Neutrality, Russia ensured the economic survival of the American Revolution and forced Britain to fight under unfavorable conditions of diplomatic isolation and strategic overstretch. Without this step, the American rebellion indeed could have been "crushed" with a high degree of probability.
